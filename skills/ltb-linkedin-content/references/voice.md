# LTB Voice Guide

The voice of Let There Be on LinkedIn: **a confident, plainspoken, warm expert.** LTB makes complex science clear — the writing should do the same thing it sells.

## The five voice principles

**1. Consumer/problem first, LTB second.**
The reader cares about their problem, not LTB's services. Open on the pain or the curiosity, then show how LTB solved it.
- ✅ "Don't make consumers read long, below-the-fold text descriptions on your eCommerce pages."
- ❌ "Let There Be is a leading science marketing agency offering eCommerce video services."

**2. Plain, declarative, short.**
Explain like you would to a smart friend who isn't a scientist. No jargon walls. Short sentences. This is the brand's core skill — demonstrate it.

**3. Specific over vague.**
Name the brand, the product, the technology, the number. Specificity signals real work and earns trust.
- ✅ "we explain the innovative lens technologies in @Bausch+Lomb's Ultra Contact Lenses"
- ❌ "we make videos for eyecare brands"

**4. Earned confidence, never hype.**
State experience and results plainly. The decade of work speaks for itself; don't oversell it.
- ✅ "After a decade of creating videos with healthcare brands, we've learned that limiting revisions is a disservice to our clients."
- ❌ "We're the #1 most innovative game-changing science marketing agency!!!"

**5. Warm and human.**
Especially on team, partnership, and conference posts. Real enthusiasm, real congratulations, real invitations to connect.

## Length & structure

- Target **150–500 characters** for most posts; the hook lives in the first ~140 (everything before "…more").
- Educational and case-study posts may run to ~1,000 characters when the substance earns it.
- One idea per post. If you have two, that's two posts.

## Emoji policy

LTB's organic LinkedIn posts use emojis **sparingly** — mostly a 🚨 flag on announcements and celebrations (promotions, partnerships). Science and case-study posts stay clean (none, or at most one). This is deliberately different from LTB's deck/skill rules, which ban emojis entirely — that ban applies to client-facing decks, not LinkedIn.

## CTAs

Soft and optional. A link, "watch the full video," or a genuine question. Never hard-sell.

## Client naming

LTB names clients with @ mentions when cleared (@Advil, @Centrum, @Bausch+Lomb, @WholisticMatters, etc.). When you're not sure a client has approved being named publicly, write the post and flag it in NOTES FOR APPROVAL rather than assuming.

## Annotated real examples

**Problem-first eCommerce (Video Showcase):**
> "Consumers buy on Amazon and other eCommerce sites to shop fast and get products fast. Don't slow them down with long, segmented text descriptions. Let There Be created this eCommerce video for @Oystershell's RID brand…"

*Why it works:* opens on consumer behavior, states the problem, then positions LTB's work as the solution. LTB appears in sentence three, not sentence one.

**Curiosity-first education (Video Showcase):**
> "Ever heard of Leaky Gut? Learn what it is and how to help improve the condition in this video we made with @WholisticMatters and @standardprocess."

*Why it works:* a question hook, a clear promise of learning, specific client tags. Short and repostable.

**Philosophy/differentiation (Benefit of LTB — use sparingly):**
> "After a decade of creating videos with healthcare brands, we've learned that limiting revisions is a disservice to our clients and the end product. Instead, Let There Be takes a 'milestones' approach…"

*Why it works:* earned confidence, a concrete point of view, no boasting. This is the rare service-adjacent post that performs, because it teaches a philosophy rather than listing a service.

**Result-first (Case Study):**
> "This Amazon BasicCare video increased sales of Hair Regrowth Treatment by 10%. Adding short explainer-style videos to your Amazon product page not only boosts page SEO, but also delivers an engaging message to consumers ABOVE the fold."

*Why it works:* the number is the hook; the body explains the mechanism so the reader learns something transferable.
